Research

I am broadly interested in questions of culture, cognition, the life course, political beliefs, and quantitative and computational methods. My principal research agenda emerges from a simple question: given the overwhelming and contradictory nature of contemporary culture, when and how do individuals maintain consistency and coherence in beliefs and behaviors over time? Should we think of people as being stable in their dispositions, or are people continually reinventing themselves as social situations change? (Or, if the answer is both, under what conditions does each model apply?) Cultural sociology presents conflicting models and expectations for when and how people will remain stable or change over time. My dissertation advances this debate by developing statistical tools to adjudicate components of these models and applying them across a wide range of survey data.

Settled Dispositions and Active Updating

The first chapter, published in the American Sociological Review (open access version here), adjudicates between two competing models of individual change that are implicit in societal-level studies of cultural change. Testing these models on more than 180 questions from the General Social Survey, I find that people tend to stop making durable changes in adulthood. These results suggest that we need a greater focus on adolescence and early adulthood if we want to understand why individuals hold specific views. An extension of this work was published in Sociological Science. I am currently working with colleagues at several universities and in several disciplines to extend this analytical framework public dimensions of culture – book publishing and music production – using computational approaches.

Ambivalence

In the second chapter, I draw on classic sociological work from Robert Merton and Neal Smelser, as well as work from political science, to develop a cultural-cognitive theory of sociological ambivalence. Using a finite mixture model approach that formalizes and quantifies three opinion behaviors – stable opinions, durable change, and vacillating change or ambivalence – in more than 500 questions from 10 panel data sets, I find that ambivalence is widespread across topic domains. Patterns of stability and instability generally reinforce the theoretical framework.

This theoretical and methodological approach provides a framework for further investigation of the sources and consequences of opinion stability and ambivalence, and I am working on several projects with a range of colleagues to explore the meso- and macro-social sources of individual attitude stability.

Measuring Schematic Culture

The widespread presence of ambivalence potentially undermines approaches to measuring culture that rely on cross-sectional data. Two related projects seek to address these issues. One project shows how prominent measures of schematic culture – Relational Class Analysis and Correlational Class Analysis – are highly inconsistent within people over time, undermining what is often the central inference of these methods. A second project shows how Latent Class Analysis, under certain assumptions, can predict which beliefs people will be consistent and inconsistent on over time, providing a new framework for understanding schematic cognition.

Culture and Computation

As a postdoctoral scholar at the University of Iowa, I am working with Yongren Shi to understand these and other dimensions of culture in online discussion communities using a variety of computational approaches.
